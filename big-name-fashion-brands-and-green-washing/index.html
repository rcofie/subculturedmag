Big-name Fashion Brands And “Green Washing”

SUBCULTUREDOctober 15, 2019
CultureFashion0 Comments

Are industry giants guilty of not living up to their claims of sustainability?

The idea of going “green” is permeating every modern market. With the textile industry being the second highest polluting industry on Earth, the word “sustainability” finds itself increasingly associated with the world of fashion. It was a recurring theme throughout London and Paris fashion weeks alike, and even fast-fashion giants such as H&M and Zara have introduced claims on sustainability.

H&M’s introduction of a Conscious range in 2018 commenced their new sustainable model, including a clothes recycling programme. However, Conscious only accounts for 5% of their total clothing output. Ann Gedda, the Head of Sustainability at H&M has said, “Big change requires bold actions and the courage to aim high”, but just how big of a change is 5%?

Alongside H&M, Zara has also made some bold claims. They have stated that by 2025, 100% of their clothing will be “more sustainable”, organic, or recycled.

The problem is that by itself, the word “sustainable” doesn’t mean anything. Big-name brands constantly throw around terms such as “green”, “eco-friendly”, and “responsibly-made”, that looks increasingly more like band-wagoning. Are corporations making genuine changes or simply making hollow statements out of societal obligation?

The problem is, the sheer vagueness of such terminology allows companies to cut corners in environmental preservation and still pander to trusting climate-conscious shoppers. The use of such words is not effectively regulated by governments on any national or international level. They are non-quantifiable adjectives that many companies see as free game, in or out of the fashion industry.  For example, the word “organic” is linked to sustainability, however, it is government mandated with a number of legal qualifiers necessary for a product to be described as such.

In fact, the word “sustainable” itself has no concrete legal definition. There are no strict legal requirements that come behind the use of the word, and no particular qualification process behind a company being able to use it. The United Kingdom Government defines “sustainability” in the 2011 policy paper Mainstreaming sustainable development: the government’s vision and what this means in practice, as the following: “The state towards which the UK Government’s vision for sustainable development aspires”.

Essentially, a meaningless phrase. Until such a time that law-makers standardise sustainability, the word will be continued to be abused by brands. This process is called greenwashing. Greenpeace’s 1999 publication “The Book of Greenwash” outlines the ways in which corporations are able to circumnavigate the United Nation’s rules on environmental impact, whilst still claiming “sustainability” and lying to the very consumers that allow them to exist.

The issue is, the entire fast-fashion business model of short sighted focus on volume, can never be built to last. The obsession with new trends on a seasonal basis allows companies choose profit margin over ethics, time after time. Even if the constant use of toxic dyes, non-recyclable fibres, animal products, copious amounts of water, overall poor quality, and worldwide shipping were all addressed, the sheer production volume and wastage rates alone produce immeasurable quantities of CO2.

Naturally, in a capitalist, ultimately materialistic society, it is impossible to exist without an environmental impact. As consumers, we cannot demand perfection of brands, but we should at least be aware of what we are actually supporting. Your next autumn outfit doesn’t need to come at the cost of supporting unethical practice.

There are a number of red flags we can use to recognise greenwashing…

  • Brands that have a conscious collection rather than the whole line. ASOS is a prime example of this with their Sustainable Edit, as well as Urban Outfitters, and Far Fetch.
  • Large corporation brands. Size does matter it seems, the larger the company, the harder it is for them to implement sustainability throughout their range. It is also easier for them to avert environmental accountability through large donations as a form of corporate purging.
  • Trend-led branding at a rapid turnover rate. In general, the Instagram-based culture of unwearable fashion for the sake of photographs is toxic for long term sustainability. 
  • The name of the brand contains a greenwashing term. Needless to say, this is the primary indicator of a brand using sustainability as a marketing gimmick.
  • Next day shipping. Overnight shipping encourages the culture of never wearing the same outfit twice, as well as expending a huge carbon footprint.  

In the words of Coco Chanel, “fashion comes and goes, style goes on forever forever”. We need to start viewing our wardrobe as a collection of investments, and not just expendable objects to discard when we grow tired of them. Buying higher quality, second hand, or from the countless truly sustainable brands available such as Patagonia, Reformation, People Tree, Boden etc., are measures we can all take to make a difference and move towards making fashion truly sustainable.
