Students In The Fight Against “Fast Fashion”

SUBCULTUREDAugust 8, 2019
Fashion0 Comments

Charity shopping, second-hand selling, and donating; what can I do in Guildford to help slow down the freight train that is the fast fashion industry? And can I really look good while doing it?

The phrase “fast fashion” has been thrown around with increasing frequency over the last few years, but what does it really mean? Apparently, the term was originally coined to describe the speedy migration of catwalk/celebrity trends to high-street retailers, with popular high-street brands such as Zara, Urban Outfitters, and H&M all promoting themselves as “fast fashion” labels. Recently, however, the term has evolved into a criticism of the very industry it was used to promote. To keep the costs down and the turnaround quick, the use of plastic-based materials, factory production, and in some cases, sweatshops, have become the norm to fuel our endless consumption.

To put things in perspective, 80 billion items of clothing are consumed worldwide each year. In 2019, we are far less likely to hold on to our old clothes than we were 10 years ago, resulting in a whopping £140 million of used, but wearable clothes being sent to landfill every year in the UK alone. To make matters worse, the fast fashion industry perpetuates itself by making clothing that aren’t made to last. Cheap materials and processes mean that our clothes are wearing out more quickly, forcing us to buy more frequently and increasing the masses of clothing sent to landfill every year.

So … what can we do to help? SUBCULTURED have some top tips for avoiding fast fashion in Guildford, as well as presenting to you six different outfits that showcase my personal favourite sustainable pieces, in a bid to convince you that a quick trip to The Friary isn’t always the best option.

This adorable outfit (minus the bag) is entirely second hand. Dungarees are a brilliantly versatile piece that can found super easily in charity or vintage shops. You can dress them up with a button-down shirt like I have here for cute, summery work-wear – or down with your favourite oversized t-shirt for a casual, 90s inspired look.

Don’t get me wrong, I’m not saying that buying or donating a few items to your local charity shop is going to immediately solve the problem that fast fashion poses, but beginning to build up a culture of second-hand shoppers will not only save resources, but also represent a massive step in forcing the fast fashion industry to reconsider their business model.

There are multiple routes to take in terms of finding clothing second hand which I am going to divide into three categories:
1. Charity Shopping
2. Online Shopping
3. Vintage Shopping

Charity Shopping

The reason I come to charity shopping first is because I know that a lot of young people are sceptical about the idea. For a long time, the charity shop was associated with grandparents and eccentrics. However, our humble Oxfams are drawing increasing crowds of environment and style conscious young people every day. The trick to charity shopping is to have patience and an open mind. A lot of people find it intimidating – the fact that there’s only one of every item, the sometimes out-dated pieces, the slightly dusty smell – but that feeling you get when you pick up an absolute gem from the rack of grandma sweaters and check the price tag (£1.99!!) is, in itself, priceless.

The point I wanted to prove with this outfit is that second hand stuff doesn’t have to be casual. I dressed up my outfit here with some sunglasses and platforms I already had in my wardrobe to create a look that I’d definitely wear on a night out.

In Guildford, there are tonnes of charity shops to check out that stock a range of items from clothing, shoes and accessories to books, music, and homewear.

You’ll need to begin at Phoenix Court to check out the Phyllis Tuckwell Hospice store.

Then, head back into White Lion Walk on North Street to visit the newly refurbished British Heart Foundation, located on the ground floor.

After that, head out across the High Street and take a trip down Quarry Street, stopping at Oxfam and the second Phyllis Tuckwell store.

Head back on yourself to take a stroll up Guildford High Street (who said shopping wasn’t valid exercise?) and dip into Save the Children which is nestled in Jeffrey’s Passage.

Continue up the high street to drop in at the Oxfam book store, Cancer Research UK, and end up at our final Oxfam, located just next to Pizza Express (phew!). After this trip (which weighs in at almost bang on 1 kilometre) you’ll at least have burned a few calories.

Since we’re right in the middle of Festival Season I thought it would be a waste not to include a fest-themed fit. For this outfit I focused on a balance between style and comfort to create a look that will stand out, but also be practical for a long day of dancing, camping, and exploring. 

Charity shopping has endless benefits, with positive implications for both the environment and the varied causes that your money is going towards. However, donating your unwanted items is also a great way to give new life to old clothes, accessories, books, and homewear, sparing them the fate of landfill. Donating is as easy as bringing a bag of items into your local store, but lots of charities also offer collection services from home, school, or work – information on which can be found here.

Online Shopping

If trekking around charity shops doesn’t fit your schedule (or just gives you a headache) online second-hand shopping has faced a massive boom in the last few years. A big increase in its popularity can be attributed to the introduction of Depop – a buying and selling app with an Instagram-like interface that has been a massive hit amongst young people.

Depop is incredibly easy to use as both a buyer and a seller, with a detailed ‘search’ mechanism with tonnes of filters, promoted sellers based on your interests, and a review system so that you know if you can really trust who you’re buying from.

Oxfam have even stepped on the trend with the creation of their own Depop account (@OxfamOnline), so that you can browse to your heart’s content without having to leave the comfort of your home.

As if we need any more options, many charity shops and vintage outlets also now have their own online shops – either independently or through the popular selling forum, Ebay – which will be linked at the end of this article.

Not to be forgotten, the student favourite ASOS also have taken a step into more sustainable fashion through the creation of ASOS Marketplace. Although it’s not guaranteed that 100% of the brands represented there are ethical and sustainable, a few that are promoting various forms of sustainability can be found here. Thanks ASOS!

If you can’t find anything you want to order, selling online is a great way to clear out your wardrobe and provide your unwanted items a new home that isn’t a landfill site. Plus, it’s a fairly easy way to make cash which can be a lifesaver for a busy (and broke) student.

When a lot of people think retro clothing, they tend to think 70s or 80s, but – nowadays – there are plenty of 90s and Y2K items in second hand stores. Since these styles are making a big comeback in mainstream fashion, I thought I’d showcase just a few of the 90s-inspired bits and pieces I’ve picked up recently to create this look.

Vintage Shopping

So, what exactly is “vintage”? According to the dictionary, the term “vintage” denotes “something from the past of high quality.” Luckily for us, vintage items have faced a massive mainstream popularity spike in recent years, with pre-loved stores such as To Be Worn Again, Beyond Retro, and Rokit paving the way. All of these stores stock tonnes of rare and beautiful second-hand pieces that will be sure to make you stand out from the crowd.

To make life even easier, the University of Surrey even hold their own vintage shopping events in collaboration with Goldrush Vintage and Yellow Jelly. Multiple times a year, Rubix is transformed into a marketplace full of quality second hand clothing from dresses and dungarees to jumpers and jeans. This is a must-see event for stylish students, so make sure to look out for the event on Facebook.

Another fun event to attend is the kilo sale up at Guildford Cathedral. Guildford has been a stop on Preloved Vintage Wholesale’s kilo sale tour for many years, so head up there, fill a bag, weigh it in and pay £15 per kilo of second hand gems! Find info and tour dates here!

For this look my main focus was wear-ability. I wanted to create a more toned-down look, inspired by streetwear, which you could easily wear to Uni or on a day out. Using small, thrifted accessories like chains, jewellery, and sunglasses can really make something as simple as jean shorts and a t-shirt look more unique and put-together. A really extra photo shoot on a tennis court also helps.

If you have a day off and fancy a trip out of town, both London and Brighton are brilliant second-hand locations. With Waterloo only 36 minutes away by train and Brighton a slightly more adventurous 1 hour 30, the shopping alone is a brilliant excuse to pay a visit. From Waterloo, be sure to take the Northern Line to Camden Town to explore the wealth of vintage shops packed full of unique fashion and curiosities. In addition, with plenty of quirky food options to be found at venues like The Cheese Bar and Rudy’s Dirty Vegan Diner, you can make a proper day of it. Find a list of London’s “best thrift stores” here, and “best charity shops” here.

In Brighton, head down to the North Laine (only a two minute walk from Brighton Station) to be in second-hand heaven. Get lost in Snooper’s Paradise, browse the rails in To Be Worn Again, and check out Dirty Harry for some filthy deals. The North Laine is also full of eateries galore, my personal favourites being the tasty We Heart Falafel; the restaurant famous for its Korean Bimibap, Namul; and the Instagram-worthy Balcony Café. If all that wasn’t enough, Brighton is also home to a ridiculous number of charity shops, the list of which have been narrowed down to a top ten here.

For my final outfit I was desperate to showcase how ridiculously cheap you can find quality items when shopping second-hand. This oversized wool cardigan and gorgeous satin dress were only £1 each at Brighton’s Shabitat, and yet they come together to create an adorably classy look.

I know, avoiding big fast fashion brands is hard – after all, they make it so easy, which I guess is sort of the point. I’m not saying that I have an entire wardrobe full of second hand clothing made out of sustainable materials … because I don’t. I love a Topshop sale from time to time, and I’m constantly being lured in by the sweet call of student discount at Urban Outfitters. However, if you can make an effort, no matter how small, to buy, sell, or donate your items second-hand, that’s a step, and with all the options as a student in Guildford – why wouldn’t you?
